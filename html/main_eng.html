Overview of Computer Networking

Introduction to Computer Networks
  • A computer network allows devices to communicate and share resources across geographical distances.
  • Networks are fundamental to the way we interact with the world today, from the internet to local area networks (LANs).
  • The basic structure of a network involves several components such as:
    • Devices (computers, smartphones, etc.)
    • Communication links (cables, wireless signals)
    • Protocols (rules for communication)
  • Understanding how networks function and are structured is crucial for managing communication systems efficiently.
Layered Network Architecture
  • The most common model for understanding computer networks is the OSI (Open Systems Interconnection) reference model, which divides networking into seven layers.
  • Each layer handles a specific aspect of networking, from physical transmission of data to application-level interactions.
  • However, in practice, the TCP/IP model, a more simplified version, is used for internet protocols.
  • This model breaks the functionality into four layers:
    • Application
    • Transport
    • Internet
    • Network Access

Examples of Computer Networks

  1. Local Area Networks (LANs)

    LANs connect devices within a limited area such as a home or office. They offer high data transfer speeds and are typically connected through wired (Ethernet) or wireless (Wi-Fi) connections.

  2. Wide Area Networks (WANs)

    WANs cover a larger geographic area, such as a city, country, or even continents. The internet is the largest and most well-known example of a WAN. WANs require high-capacity communication links, often using fiber-optic cables or satellite connections to manage vast amounts of data traffic.
